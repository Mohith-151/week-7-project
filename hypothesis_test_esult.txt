==========================================================
HYPOTHESIS TESTING REPORT: SALES & MARKETING ANALYSIS
==========================================================

TEST 1: NORMALITY TEST (SHAPIRO-WILK)
----------------------------------------------------------
- Variable: Daily_Revenue
- Null Hypothesis (H0): Daily Revenue follows a normal distribution.
- Alternative Hypothesis (H1): Daily Revenue does not follow a normal distribution.
- Result: p-value = 0.000 (Significant)
- Conclusion: Reject H0. The data is non-normal (Bimodal Distribution). 
- Impact: Non-parametric tests (Mann-Whitney U) will be used for group comparison.

TEST 2: 'PROMO_STRATEGY' COMPARISON (MANN-WHITNEY U)
----------------------------------------------------------
- Comparison: Standard Strategy vs. Aggressive_Discount Strategy
- Null Hypothesis (H0): There is no significant difference in revenue between the two strategies.
- Alternative Hypothesis (H1): The Aggressive_Discount strategy results in significantly higher revenue.
- Test Statistic: 0.0 (U-statistic)
- Result: p-value ≈ 4.98e-33 (approx 0.000000000000000000000000000000000000048)
- Alpha Level: 0.05
- Conclusion: Reject H0. The difference is statistically significant.
- Business Interpretation: The promotion strategy has a massive impact on revenue that cannot be attributed to random chance.

TEST 3: CORRELATION SIGNIFICANCE
----------------------------------------------------------
- Variables: Marketing_Spend & Daily_Revenue
- Method: Spearman Rank Correlation (due to non-normality)
- Result: ρ~ 0.999
- Conclusion: 
    - Strong positive monotonic relationship. 
    - Increasing marketing spend consistently leads to higher revenue across both strategy groups.

SUMMARY OF FINDINGS
----------------------------------------------------------
The analysis confirms with near-absolute statistical certainty that the Aggressive_Discount strategy outperforms the Standard strategy.
The non-normal distribution of revenue is explained by the distinct performance gap between these two operational modes.
